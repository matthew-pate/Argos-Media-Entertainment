Hazards of Love at 10

17 March 2019

The Decemberists' Hazards Of Love was made available on iTunes 10 years ago today, March 17, 2009. The record was released a week later on March 24, 2009.

It wasn’t unexpected. It wasn’t shockingly different from what we had heard in the past. Fans of The Decemberists didn’t mind the work it required to unfurl the lyrics, they had enjoyed the constant dictionary consults and imagery in the past. In fact, I’ll require their discography as required listening for my daughter – when she’s old enough – to increase her own vocabulary.

It was, in some respects, a line in the sand.

It was the type of album that one would aspire to create – if they aspired to create a rock-opera reminiscent of Led Zepplin, Pink Floyd, or The Who. Complete with a children’s choir which conjurede thoughts of The Rolling Stones, The Hazards Of Love, was most nearly a signal that The Crane Wife, not Picaresque, would be their last indie record. While Picaresque was the last to be released on an indie label, Kill Rock Stars, Crane Wife certainly held much closer to the band’s roots.

Unlike many of the bands during this era that had made the indie-to-major-label jump, The Decemberists had been able to maintain their sound and following. They proved with both, Crane Wife and Hazards, that a band did not have to sacrifice their sound, their fanbase, or their roots to find further success on a major label.

“…if you hate sweeping tales that invoke mythology and fantasy, you’re not going to find much to like….On The Crane Wife, the band’s previous LP, songs varied between conventional acoustic rock tracks…and indie epics…. Although that was the Portland-based band’s first big label release, it proved that they could keep their literate, albeit esoteric, style and make it accessible to a wide audience.” – Alex Young, CoS

As the first decade of the 21st century was coming to a close, where iPods ran on shuffle and streaming was beginning its massive explosion, The Decemberists released an album which nearly required the listener to follow the story from “Prelude” to “The Hazards Of Love 4 (The Drowned).” It was 2009’s The Wall, and your college roommate most assuredly forced you to listen to the entire 17 track, hour-long rock-opera from start to finish without a break. It wouldn’t be until years later that you’d actually return to the album to truly take it in.

While elements of past albums reappear, they do less for Hazards than one might expect. The shape-shifting creatures, the tales of rape, children, and murder are all there, but the album lacks the one-liners and hooks that made Castaways and Cutouts and Her Majesty the Decemberists such great, iconic albums. I recall walking the halls of my middle and high schools with “July, July!,” “The Soldiering Life,” and “Red Right Ankle,” among others spinning in my head.

“In an interview with Paste, singing guitarist/songwriter Colin Meloy mentioned that The Hazards of Love was ‘initially conceived as a musical…but I decided about halfway through my time in France that it wasn’t going to work as a stage piece. But it would still work as a rock record….'” – Marc Hogan, Pitchfork

Agreeably, where Hazards failed as a musical, may be exactly where it fails as a record.

Long-time listeners were provided few “treasures” — as my daughter would call them. Tracks like “The Wanting Comes In Waves/Repaid,” “The Hazards Of Love 4 (The Drowned),” “Margaret In Captivity,” and few others are our tokens for being benevolent listeners and fans.

Long-time listeners respected the discipline that this and past albums have taken to create, especially given how each track bleeds into the next. Those who loved the story depicted in Crane Wife will enjoy this continued narrative style. Those of us who were looking for more sea chanteys would have to look to the past and pray for the future.

Where the album truly won was in its story — the epic. While some claimed that the story, lyricism, and composition work better in Hazards than in The Who’s Tommy, specifically, “Pinball Wizard,” I must disagree.

I further disagree with Drowned In Sound‘s Alexander Tudor‘s assumption that Hazards “could bring more fans than ever before, because the constraints of the rock-opera mean that Meloy’s writing some of his simplest, most immediately affecting songs.” While the lyricism is certainly more accessible, your average or mainstream listener isn’t typically interested in deep, thought-provoking tales of rape, murder, or even love. The discipline it took to create the album, I would argue, is proportional to the discipline it takes to listen to the album.

In an age of singles — shuffling tracks and streaming services — rock-operas and full length albums need to adapt. While it’s an amazing effort, more accessible lyricism doesn’t generate hour-long listeners. Unless, of course, you’re gearing up for a particular show and want your lip-syncing to be on point.

There’s no denying that the album requires your full attention and a good hour and a half — if not more — to to reflect.

And, since we’re living in the future, I can break the news to everyone: The future is bright. It has been 10 years and, as Colin Meloy sings at the close of the rock-opera, “these hazards of love, never more will trouble us.”
