Without Consequence, Without Regret

21 February 2019

In a debut album riddled with purposeful responses to those who claimed she’d never make it in the industry, Alice Merton‘s strong-willed and persevering lyrics are impaired by what may be assumed as her insecurities. As the bass hits, those impactful moments are narrowly missed or avoided. It’s almost as if Merton’s search for a “home” can be felt even in the denial that it is underway. Merton can be heard fighting to live on her own terms and timing while borrowing from others.

Prior to MINT, titled for Merton’s stress-relieving aid, I had only experienced the artist through radio plays of “No Roots,” “Lash Out,” and an endearing set played at Austin City Limits Music Festival (ACL). The radio plays concerned me — “No Roots” had been exhausted and “Lash Out,” though it had come as a breath of fresh air, was following suit.

At ACL, my wife and I found ourselves at Merton’s set out of respect for the radio plays and due to the lack of other performers scheduled during Merton’s time slot. It was a pleasant surprise. If this were a review of Merton’s ACL performance, it would certainly be much more positive. Her interaction with the audience and her story-telling between songs was among the best I had seen in years at the festival.

MINT is filled with peaks and valleys. The unfortunate truth: The peaks don’t deliver and the valleys leave the listener wanting – expecting meaning that was never flushed out or entrusted to the listener.

The opening track, “Learn To Live,” propels the album’s expectation established by both, “No Roots” and “Lash Out.” It also lays out Merton’s primary objective: To live without consequences. Yet, somewhat unexpectedly, the track is followed by the tale of a chance meeting at a bus stop with an approach similar to that of Tegan & Sara.

“No Roots” quickly became overplayed and “Lash Out” was a breath of fresh air when it entered the daily line-up. Although the track had been exhausted, it wasn’t until I listened closer that I truly heard Merton’s claim, or realization, that “[her] home was never on the ground.” It’s interesting how one can be exhausted by a track, but find a connection through the lyricism – regardless of the level of complexity.

It’s not until after these introductory tracks that we come upon my favorites of the album, “Funny Business” and “Homesick.” Paired with a beat that you can tap your heel to, a clap-able rhythm, and a catchy hook – “I don’t break hearts / I don’t do funny business / I just ride in cars and watch them get hung up in it” – make “Funny Business” an easy favorite and eases the listener into the piano-heavy, “Homesick”, brimming with influences such as Regina Spektor and Sara Bareilles. Repetitiously, she sings, “I don’t feel alone-lone-lone-lone-lone-lone-lone-lone-lone.” As if she is either proud to have found someone or simply trying to make herself believe it’s true.

In many ways, MINT is an album for nomads – anyone who has experienced a life of goodbyes. Whether it be physically moving away, emotionally leaving others, or missed opportunities.

In a recent interview, Merton stated that she felt, “like [her] identity has definitely been carved from the fact that [she] keep[s] on saying goodbye.”

In the same interview Merton expresses that she is “so bad at making up [her] mind” because she has “a thousand thoughts,” but that it’s part of her identity. She connects with those who call themselves nomads or refuse to claim a home as she has “never been able to put [her] feet in the ground.” She explains that “they’re on the ground, but they’re never in it. And it really annoys [her] sometimes.”

“Lash Out” carries a heavy bass line and is festival ready – I know, I was there. It resembles The Ting Tings‘ “Shut Up and Let Me Go” and “That’s Not My Name.”

With all the obvious influences, it’s easy to spot a Canadian influence. Alanis Morissett can be heard in many tracks, even the softer “Speak your Mind.” Even here, we see where the lyricism, while rather simple, can overpower the composition when given its due consideration.

Another favorite, “I Don’t Hold A Grudge” is a solid track. Had dance steps been included, it would provide stiff competition to “The Scarn.” And that’s a compliment. Fans of Wild Child will immediately enjoy “Honeymoon Heartbreak” with it’s folk-turned-rock aesthetics.

The album is sprinkled with undertones of Fiona Apple and akin to Florence & The Machine. At the end of the album, I’m still left wanting more, more depth, more detail, more bass, more treble. Just, more.

It’s the final track that really speaks – of childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and the future.

“…I wanna live in the now / I’m not gonna live in the past / Don’t wanna care about what-ifs / ‘Cause what if those things don’t last? / And I wanna live with no regrets / I wanna live in the now / I’m not gonna live in the past….”
